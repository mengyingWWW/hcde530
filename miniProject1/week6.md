## Chart 1 — `chart_q1_low_star_share_by_app.png`

**MP1a question:** What usability complaints appear consistently across all three AI apps, and which pain points are unique to a single product?

**What this chart shows:** A **bar chart** (categorical comparison) of the **percentage of reviews rated 1–2 stars** for each app, pooling both stores. That is a **quantitative proxy for visible dissatisfaction** before full thematic coding of “complaints.”

**Chart-type fit:** The course guide maps **categorical comparison → bar chart**. One categorical axis (app) and one numeric outcome (percent) matches `px.bar`.

**Design choices:** The vertical axis starts at **0%** so differences in bar height are not exaggerated. Axis labels state **percent** and the title states the star band (1–2). I did **not** claim this chart *is* thematic usability analysis—only that it shows **where low-star volume concentrates** in this scrape (Claude shows the highest share in this sample).

## Chart 2 — `chart_q2_mean_rating_by_recency_decile.png`

**MP1a question:** Did average user ratings shift following major app updates, and what themes appear in reviews from those time windows?

**Why not calendar weeks:** We pulled a **fixed number of reviews per app**, but each app’s **newest-to-oldest calendar span** differed (ChatGPT’s 1,000 reviews clustered in a few days; others stretched back weeks). Plotting **mean rating by calendar week** therefore compared **different slices of “the scrape”** and could show **one point** for an app even though the sample is large.

**What this chart shows instead:** A **line chart** with **ordinal position on the x-axis**: within each app, reviews are sorted **newest first**, split into **10 equal-count deciles** (decile **1** = newest 10% of that app’s rows in this file, **10** = oldest 10%). The y-axis is still **mean star rating (1–5)**. All three apps now share the **same x-axis meaning** (“depth into this scrape”), which is fairer for a controlled row count.

**Chart-type fit:** The Week 6 guide pairs **line charts with numeric progression**; here the x-axis is **discrete deciles** (1–10) in order, which reads as a **stepped trend** across the scrape—reasonable for “does sentiment drift across the window we collected?” without claiming exact calendar alignment.

**Design choices:** **Y-axis fixed 0–5** stars. The title states that deciles are **within each app’s sample**, not tied to **App Store release dates**; linking to “major updates” still needs version/release metadata and text themes in MP1b.

**Limitation:** This answers **gradient within the fetched stack**, not “after version 1.2 shipped on May 1.”

## Chart 3 — `chart_q3_keyword_themes_1_vs_5_star.png`

**MP1a question:** How do 5-star and 1-star reviewers describe the same core features (e.g. accuracy, speed, conversation quality) differently?

**What this chart shows:** A **grouped bar chart** comparing **1-star vs 5-star** reviews on **pilot keyword themes** (simple case-insensitive substring patterns for speed/responsiveness, accuracy/trust, and memory/context language). The height is the **percent of reviews in that star bucket** that mention each theme.

**Chart-type fit:** This is still a **categorical comparison** (theme × star bucket), so **grouped bars** are appropriate; it is not a scatter (two continuous variables) or a raw distribution histogram.

**Design choices:** The y-axis starts at **0%**. The title says **“pilot keyword”** to signal that this is **not** full NLP or validated codebooks—MP1b can replace this with richer thematic coding.

## Competency claim

I used **Plotly** with **honest axes** (full **0–5** scale where star means are shown; **0%** baselines for share charts), **clear titles** that state what is and is not claimed, and **axis labels with units** (percent, star scale, decile index). I matched **chart types to data structure** using the Week 6 guide (bar for categorical comparisons, line for trends along an ordered axis, grouped bars for theme comparisons). I connected each figure to a **different MP1a question** while documenting where the visualization is only a **first pass** (themes, releases, and cross-app “consistent complaints” still need text work in MP1b). Exporting static **PNG** files with **kaleido** shows I can move from interactive exploration to **portfolio-ready artifacts** in the repo.
